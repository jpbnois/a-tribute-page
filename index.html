Introduction:

Jackie Robinson, born January 13 1919, was a second basemen who became the first African American to play in the major leagues in the modern era. Robinson broke the baseball color line when the Brooklyn Dodgers started him at first base on April 15, 1947. The Dodgers, by playing Robinson, heralded the end of racial segregation that had relegated black players to the Negro leagues since the 1880s. Robinson was inducted into the Baseball Hall of Fame in 1962.

Jackie Robinson, sliding, in his most iconic photo where he steals home plate by Yogi Berra in the 1955 World Series.

Jackie Robinson's early life and millitary career:

  • Jackie Robinson came from a big family. Robinson was the youngest of five children and was raised by a single mother.
  • In high school, Robinson was an all-star athlete and excelled in football, basketball, track, and baseball.
  • Robinson’s older brother was an Olympic silver-medal winner in the 200-metre dash.
  • From 1942 to 1944, the soon-to-be baseball legend served as a second lieutenant in World War II, but was never involved in combat. He was later arrested during boot camp because he refused to sit at the back of a segregated bus.
  • After returning from WWII, Robinson accepted a position an old friend, pastor Rev. Karl Downs. Robinson became the athletic director at Sam Huston College in Austin, where he coached basketball for the 1944–45 season.

April 1947, Jackie Robinson, first from the right - poses in a photo with his Brooklyn Dodger teammates at Ebbets Field in Brooklyn, New York.

Jackie Robinson's MLB career, social activism and philantrophy:

  • The Hall-of-Famer first started his baseball career in the Negro League with the Kansas City Monarchs.
  • After being called up by Branch Rickey, who was the president of the Brooklyn Dodgers, Robinson became the first African-American in the MLB system, breaking the six decade-long racial segregation in baseball and contributed to the Civil Rights Movement.
  • Robinson made his AAA debut with the Montreal Royals Daytona Beach’s City Island Ballpark on March 17, 1946, which was an exhibition game against parent club, the Dodgers. Robinson was called up to the Dodgers six days before the 1947 season.
  • Robinson’s criticisms towards segregation made numerous establishments change their rules.
  • After retiring, Robinson was diagnosed with Diabetes.
  • After his death, Robinson’s widow created the Jackie Robinson Foundation, which, according to the Foundation’s site, “provides a host of networking opportunities with mentors and peers from around the country — inspiring leaders and building character.”

Memorable quotes from Jackie Robinson:

"Baseball is like a poker game. Nobody wants to quit when he's losing; nobody wants you to quit when you're ahead." - Jackie Robinson on the internal struggles of a baseball player.
"But as I write these words now I cannot stand and sing the National Anthem. I have learned that I remain a black in a white world." - Jackie Robinson on White Supremacy in 1950s America.
"Life is not a spectator sport. If you're going to spend your whole life in the grandstand just watching what goes on, in my opinion you're wasting your life." - Jackie Robinson on living vicariously through athletes.
